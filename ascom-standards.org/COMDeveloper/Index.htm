Driver Developer Resources

Once you have become familiar with the ASCOM ecosystem (see above), look over the COM Driver Design topics and the COM Driver Practical Issues in the orange menu. Most of the driver developer tools are contained within the ASCOM Platform Developer Components. Driver developers are strongly encouraged to use those tools. This is particularly true for driver developers who write in C# or VB.NET. The Developer Components contain templates for all driver types in both C# and VB.NET. Using these templates will save you heaps of effort and troubles.

You may benefit from watching Tom How's excellent video showing how to develop a driver for a telescope control system using the popular Arduino microcontroller kit.

Developer Components (separate download)

Before embarking on driver development, you should have the ASCOM Platform Developer Components installed on your system. Driver templates, most of the developer documentation, and the installer generator are included in the Developer Components installation. The developer components are provided as a separate download from the Platform itself.

Conformance Checker

See Conformance Testing. Once you have your driver working, it's very important to test it with the Driver Conformance Checker tool. This tool will exercise your driver and the device it handles, checking for common programming errors.

Role of Simulators

The ASCOM Platform includes simulators for each of the devices covered by an ASCOM standard. These simulators provide a convenient tool for application software developers to test their programs with known good drivers under controlled conditions. The simulators also serve driver developers as reference implementations of the driver standards. If there is a question about the behavior of a property or method, the behavior of the appropriate simulator serves as the reference.

Common Traps and Mistakes (mostly for driver developers)

It probably seems unprofessional to start this section with what not to do, but we're just trying to save you time and pain. Over the last 12+ years we've seen the same mistakes repeated by driver developers enough times to rate being listed here. Hopefully a word to the wise is enough.

  • Not understanding the driver-client architecture and that your driver will be used by many applications, some of which don't yet exist. See About Alpaca and ASCOM for the basics.
  • Exposing timing restrictions or other device quirks to driver clients/apps (they don't want to do stuff just for your needy device). See The General Principle under the COM Developer info. The principles also apply to Alpaca.
  • Using sources for another driver (or worse, one of the simulators) as a starting point (you start with other peoples' mistakes and the wrong design)
  • Developing for both 32-bit and 64-bit using "any cpu" in .NET, yet depending on an external library that is 32-bit only (C++ DLL for 32-bits etc.).
  • Insisting on using C++ because that's the only language you know, but you know nothing about COM (bare metal COM in C++ is tough)
  • Ignoring the support services provided by the Platform (Chooser, Profile, Serial, etc.) and rolling your own
  • Asking users to run BAT files or double-click registry files to install your driver (instead of providing a real installer using our tools)
  • Including (and worse, installing) parts taken from the Platform as part of your driver or applicaition
